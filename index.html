GFBS

Goals For Better Software

Introduction

Software development is a craft. We incrementally improve as we learn from our own experience and the experience of others to build that next piece of software. There is no "best", because there is always "better". What qualifies as "better" is the subject of a vast number of books, acronyms, tools, and opinions. Propagating this knowledge is traditionally very time consuming, and a real challenge to scale to the needs of teams and organizations.

The hypothesis of GFBS is that working toward a minimal set of goals will cause the majority of "better" software development practices to emerge as a byproduct. A set of experiments will help to close the gaps between goals and reality. The experiments will also let us know the distance to the goal, and can spark great conversations within a team.

Better Code

Goal: Naming

"Indeed, the ratio of time spent reading versus writing is well over 10 to 1. We are constantly reading old code as part of the effort to write new code. ...[Therefore,] making it easy to read makes it easier to write.", Robert C. Martin, Clean Code

We choose names for classes, functions, variables, etc to communicate knowledge and intent. Better names are more quickly and easily understood by a larger audience. Conversely, worse names require more time and experience to understand. As Uncle Bob suggests, we should optimize reading because that is where we spend the majority of our time. The size of the audience is important because it is easier to scale the number of contributors with a larger pool.

Experiment: Widen The Audience

We want to learn how much time and experience is needed to understand a piece of code. Junior developers are the best collaborators because their feedback is the most likely to increase the size of the audience. In-person or video conferencing works best because facial expressions, body language, and muttered or exclaimed wtf's are often filtered when using text-based tools.

Pair up with a collaborator and sit down with your code. Ask them to explain what the code is doing and why. Unless the person is completely stuck, listen and do not guide them! Misunderstandings and insights often show areas for improvement where we can choose better names for classes, functions, and variables, etc.
